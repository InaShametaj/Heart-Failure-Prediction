💡 Conclusion & Insights

The supervised learning pipeline applied to the Heart Disease dataset yields five observations that together describe both the dataset's characteristics and the relative strengths of the four models evaluated.
The dataset is clean and moderately balanced, with 918 patient records, no missing values, and a 55/45 split between positive and negative heart disease cases. This balance means no resampling is required and all standard classification metrics can be interpreted without correction. The five categorical features — chest pain type, resting ECG, ST slope, sex, and exercise angina — require encoding before modelling, expanding the feature space from 12 to 19 columns, and each of these encoded features contributes meaningfully to at least one of the trained models.
The Decision Tree offers the most interpretable output of all four models. By limiting depth to four levels, the tree remains readable while capturing the most important clinical decision boundaries. ST slope, chest pain type, and oldpeak consistently emerge as the top splits, reflecting their well-established clinical relevance to heart disease diagnosis. The plotted tree makes the model's logic fully transparent — a significant advantage in a medical context where decisions must be explainable to practitioners.
Logistic Regression typically outperforms Linear Regression on this task because it is mathematically suited to binary outcomes. Linear Regression produces predictions outside the 0–1 range and makes no probabilistic guarantee, while Logistic Regression outputs calibrated probabilities through the sigmoid function. The coefficient analysis from Logistic Regression further confirms which features are the strongest positive and negative predictors of disease, offering a level of interpretability second only to the Decision Tree.
Naïve Bayes performs competitively despite its strong and largely violated independence assumption. Clinical measurements such as age, cholesterol, maximum heart rate, and resting blood pressure are correlated with one another, yet Gaussian Naïve Bayes still produces reasonable predictions because the marginal distributions per class are sufficiently distinct. Its speed and simplicity make it a useful baseline, particularly when data is limited or a probabilistic output is needed quickly.
Across all models, recall on the positive class is the metric that matters most in this context. A missed heart disease diagnosis carries far greater clinical risk than a false alarm — a patient incorrectly told they are healthy may forgo treatment that could be life-saving. The model selected for deployment should therefore be the one that maximises recall on the positive class, even at a modest cost to overall accuracy or precision.
